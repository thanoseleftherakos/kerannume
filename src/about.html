It has been customary for some years now for physicists of the theoretical kind to argue for the illusion of time. Whether they are right or wrong my knowledge does not permit me to say yet. I cannot come to refute it. Yet, although I know that my immediate experience cannot be the absolute arbiter of truth, I, at least, can surmise, having been all my life nothing else but a practical man, that immediate experience must be the first but not the only of tools used by man in the process of verification.

I am a fairly poor man and inured to it. Whatever I am now has been partly determined by this condition; either as a vertiginous reaction to it and a putrefying subsumption by it or as a progressive negation and coalescence of it – I am not sure where I tend more to have been throughout my life. Although the paucity of my knowledge, which is a consequence of my condition, does not allow me to judge a number of things in their intricate details, it does not, however, preclude me from wonderment and inquiry and as a consequence from participating in the jury of truth.
